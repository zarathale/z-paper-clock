What this is

This site is the public face of a personal hobby project — a digital reinterpretation of James Smith Rudolph's Make Your Own Working Paper Clock (Harper & Row, 1983; in print as a Perennial reprint). The book is a set of thirteen plates containing 123 pieces that, when cut, folded, and assembled, form a working pendulum clock made entirely of cardstock.

The project here is a 3D digital model of that clock — every piece traced from scans of an authentic copy, every fold and glue point preserved, the whole assembly rebuilt as an interactive web object. It's done in the open, in collaboration with Claude, an AI from Anthropic. The collaboration is part of the point.

A note on scope: the book itself is held privately for personal reference and isn't republished here. If you'd like to build the physical clock, please buy a copy — it's still in print.

The pieces

There are 123 pieces in the clock. Most are panels of folded cardstock; some are mechanism components — gears, an anchor, a pendulum, a weight. Each is being hand-traced into a layered SVG and given a small JSON sidecar that records its cuts, folds, glue points, and (for the moving parts) the geometry the mechanism actually needs to tick.

The collaboration

The project is being built by Claude — an AI from Anthropic — working on Alan's behalf. The split is unusual: Claude leads, Alan enables. Alan owns the physical-side of the digital model. He runs the scanner, hand-authors the per-piece SVGs in Affinity Designer, and his memory of how the clock actually folds is the source of truth. Claude owns the rest — the queue of what gets authored next, the per-piece sidecars, the pipeline, the eventual viewer code, this site.

Because the working agreement is unusual, it's also written down. The collaboration charter is public. So is every architectural decision, every working note, the queue Alan pulls from, and the dropbox where Claude leaves piece-revisions for him to act on. The repo is a working laboratory; the site is its window.

What's next

The headline goal is a public 3D viewer of the assembled clock — orbit, zoom, explode it into individual pieces, click any piece to see how it folds. That ships in stages: first a flat viewer (every piece, no assembly), then assembled, then (as a stretch goal) the mechanism actually animates at real-time pace.

Today, the build is at the per-piece authoring stage — slower than viewer work but the bottleneck for everything downstream. The site grows alongside it; what's on this page is what exists today, and it'll evolve as the build does.
